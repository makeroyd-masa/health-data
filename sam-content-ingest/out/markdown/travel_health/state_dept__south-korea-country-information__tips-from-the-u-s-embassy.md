# South Korea Country Information — Tips from the U.S. embassy

- [The Korean Electronic Travel Authorization (K-ETA)](https://www.k-eta.go.kr/portal/newapply/index.do) exemption was extended through December 31, 2026. K-ETA is required starting January 1, 2027.
- Some prescription drugs are illegal in South Korea and others require pre-approval from the [South Korean Ministry of Food and Drug Safety (K-MFDS)](https://www.mfds.go.kr/brd/m_74/view.do?seq=43906&srchFr=&srchTo=&srchWord=%EB%A7%88%EC%95%BD&srchTp=0&itm_seq_1=0&itm_seq_2=0&multi_itm_seq=0&company_cd=&company_nm=&Data_stts_gubun=C9999&page=1). If traveling with amphetamines, narcotics, or opioids, you must have proper authorization.
- Men with dual South Korean and U.S. citizenships, including U.S. service members,  may be required to serve in the South Korean military. U.S. citizens with family ties to South Korea should consult the South Korean embassy in the United States before traveling. Review the information from the [Korean Military Manpower Administration](https://www.mma.go.kr/eng/index.do).
- Foreigners cannot participate in political activities, like protests, under [South Korean immigration law](https://elaw.klri.re.kr/eng_mobile/viewer.do?hseq=57464&type=part&key=7%22HYPERLINK%20%22https://elaw.klri.re.kr/eng_mobile/viewer.do?hseq=57464&type=part&key=7).
- International child custody disputes can be challenging to resolve in South Korea. Parents with custody concerns should review [South Korea’s record on international parental child abduction](https://travel.state.gov/content/travel/en/International-Parental-Child-Abduction/International-Parental-Child-Abduction-Country-Information/KoreaRepublicof.html).
