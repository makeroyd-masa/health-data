# Antarctica — Vaccines and Medicines

All travelers should be up-to-date on [routine vaccines](/travel/diseases/routine). These vaccines include measles-mumps-rubella (MMR) vaccine, diphtheria-tetanus-pertussis vaccine, varicella (chickenpox) vaccine, polio vaccine, and your yearly flu shot. The risk of [flu](/travel/diseases/influenza-seasonal-zoonotic-and-pandemic), [measles](/travel/diseases/measles), [mumps](/travel/diseases/mumps), and other diseases easily spread from person-to-person is related to travel and living conditions. People from all over the world may travel to Antarctica on [cruise ships](/travel/page/cruise-ship). Sharing close living quarters with people from many different places increases the risk of exposure to illnesses.

All eligible travelers should be up to date with their [COVID-19](/travel/diseases/covid19) vaccines. Please see [Your COVID-19 Vaccination](https://www.cdc.gov/coronavirus/2019-ncov/vaccines/your-vaccination.html) for more information.

Other travel vaccines should be considered according to what [countries you travel through](/travel/destinations/list) on your way to Antarctica.

Visit your doctor (ideally, 4–6 weeks) before your trip to get vaccines or medicines you may need.
